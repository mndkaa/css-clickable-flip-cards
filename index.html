LE LOUVRE

1793

A visit to the Louvre and its collections lets visitors discover Western art from the Middle Ages to 1848, as well as a large number of ancient civilizations. Yet it also offers another history to explore. The grand palace that houses the museum, which dates back to the late twelfth century, is a true lesson in architecture: from 1200 to 2011, the most innovative architects have in turn built and developed the Louvre. Long the seat of power, this royal residence was also home to French heads of state until 1870 and is one of the major backdrops to the history of Paris and of France..

highlights:
  1. Mona Lisa: Leonardo da Vinci's famous masterpiece, the Mona Lisa, is undoubtedly the most iconic and visited artwork in the Louvre. Its enigmatic smile and meticulous technique make it a must-see for visitors from around the world.
  2. Venus de Milo: This ancient Greek statue of the goddess Aphrodite, known as the Venus de Milo, is one of the most renowned sculptures in the Louvre. It is admired for its graceful pose, beautiful craftsmanship, and timeless elegance.
  3. Winged Victory of Samothrace: The Winged Victory of Samothrace is a magnificent Hellenistic sculpture that depicts Nike, the Greek goddess of victory. Its dramatic composition and dynamic stance make it a captivating masterpiece.
  4. The Great Sphinx of Tanis: Located in the Egyptian Antiquities section, the Great Sphinx of Tanis is a remarkable sculpture that dates back to the 26th century BCE. It represents a lion with a human head and serves as a testament to ancient Egyptian civilization.
  5. Code of Hammurabi: This ancient Babylonian law code, carved on a large stele, is one of the world's oldest preserved legal texts. It provides a glimpse into the laws and social structure of ancient Mesopotamia.

Metropolitan Museum

1870

The Metropolitan Museum of Art of New York City, colloquially “the Met”, is the largest art museum in the United States. Presenting over 5,000 years of art from around the world for everyone to experience and enjoy, the museum lives in two iconic sites in New York City: The Met Fifth Avenue and The Met Cloisters. Since its founding in 1870, The Met has always aspired to be more than a treasury of rare and beautiful objects. Every day, art comes alive in the museum’s galleries and through its exhibitions and events, revealing new ideas and unexpected connections across time and across cultures.

highlights:
  1. The Temple of Dendur: This ancient Egyptian temple, dating back to the first century BCE, is one of the most impressive installations at the Met. It was relocated from its original location in Egypt and reconstructed in the museum, providing visitors with a unique experience of ancient Egyptian architecture.
  2. The Met Cloisters: Located in Upper Manhattan, the Met Cloisters is a branch of the Metropolitan Museum of Art dedicated to medieval art and architecture. It houses a stunning collection of medieval artworks, including the famous Unicorn Tapestries, stained glass windows, and illuminated manuscripts.
  3. The Metropolitan Museum of Art Costume Institute: The Met's Costume Institute is renowned for its extensive collection of fashion and costume artifacts. It hosts the annual Met Gala, a highly anticipated event in the fashion world, and showcases rotating exhibitions that explore the intersection of art and fashion.
  4. European Paintings: The Met's European Paintings collection boasts an exceptional range of masterpieces from renowned artists. Highlights include works by Vincent van Gogh, Claude Monet, Pablo Picasso, Rembrandt, and Johannes Vermeer, among others.
  5. The American Wing: Dedicated to American art and culture, the American Wing houses a diverse collection of paintings, sculptures, furniture, and decorative arts from various periods. Notable works include Emanuel Leutze's iconic painting "Washington Crossing the Delaware" and John Singer Sargent's portrait of Madame X.

The British Museum

1753

The British Museum in London was founded in 1753 and opened its doors in 1759. It was the first national museum to cover all fields of human knowledge, open to visitors from across the world. No other museum is responsible for collections of the same depth and breadth, beauty and significance. Its eight million objects allow us to explore the extraordinary diversity of human cultures, from small communities to vast empires, to discover the many forms and expressions human beings have given to every aspect of life, and to realize how closely they are interconnected

highlights:
  1. Rosetta Stone: The Rosetta Stone is one of the British Museum's most famous and prized possessions. It is an ancient Egyptian artifact that played a crucial role in deciphering hieroglyphs. Visitors can marvel at this significant archaeological discovery.
  2. Elgin Marbles: The Elgin Marbles, also known as the Parthenon Marbles, are a collection of classical Greek sculptures and friezes that once adorned the Parthenon temple in Athens. They showcase the skill and beauty of ancient Greek art and are a major highlight of the British Museum.
  3. Egyptian Mummies: The British Museum houses a remarkable collection of Egyptian mummies and artifacts. Visitors can explore the intricacies of ancient Egyptian burial practices and gain insights into the beliefs and customs of this fascinating civilization.
  4. Sutton Hoo Treasure: The Sutton Hoo Treasure is a collection of Anglo-Saxon artifacts discovered in an archaeological site in Suffolk, England. The treasure includes intricate jewelry, weaponry, and other objects, providing a glimpse into the early medieval history of Britain.
  5. Assyrian Lion Hunt Reliefs: These impressive stone reliefs depict scenes of lion hunting and warfare from the ancient Assyrian Empire. They showcase the prowess and power of the Assyrian kings and offer a glimpse into ancient Mesopotamian culture.
